Bias Protected characteristic Proxy variable Direct discrimination Indirect discrimination Fairness metric
Bias – Systematic errors that cause a model to treat groups differently. Usually arises because the training data reflects human prejudice or skewed representation – "bias in, bias out".
Protected characteristic – Attributes that anti-discrimination law forbids using as a basis for decisions, e.g. gender, ethnicity, age, religion and disability.
Proxy variable – A variable so closely correlated with a protected characteristic (e.g. gender or ethnicity) that a model can use it as a substitute – thereby disadvantaging a group without ever seeing the protected characteristic directly. A CV employment gap can become a proxy for gender; a postcode can become a proxy for ethnicity.
Direct discrimination – The decision changes when only the protected characteristic changes. The twin test reveals this: same qualifications, different outcome.
Indirect discrimination – An apparently neutral criterion (e.g. "no gaps in CV") that in practice disadvantages one group far more than another.
Fairness metric – A way of measuring fairness. This lab uses demographic parity: the share recommended should be roughly equal across groups when qualifications are equal. The gap is measured in percentage points.
In 2018 it was revealed that Amazon had scrapped an internal AI tool for recruitment. The tool had been trained on ten years of historical applications – which came predominantly from men – and learned to downgrade CVs that suggested the applicant was a woman, for example through the names of societies or associations.

Amazon tried to remove the obvious signals, but could not guarantee the model would not find other, indirect routes to the same conclusion. A variable that smuggles a protected characteristic in through the back door in this way is called a proxy variable.

Note: In this lab the proxy variable is intentionally fairly obvious. In reality it can be much harder to detect which variables are acting as proxies.

The Bias Lab

An AI learns from data. But what happens when that data reflects historical injustices? Here you get to see first-hand how discrimination can creep into an AI system.

In 2018 Amazon was forced to scrap an AI recruitment tool that had learned to downgrade women's CVs. In this lab you build a similar model for the fictional company Technova Inc. and investigate how it could happen. Technova trains its AI on its own past hiring decisions, but historically men were hired far more often than women despite equivalent qualifications.

1
Train and inspect – Let the model learn from the historical data. Then see what it has learned, how it evaluates new applicants, and what it answers when given two identical CVs that differ only in gender.
2
Try to fix it – Hide the applicants' gender from the model and investigate whether that is enough to make it fair.

💡 All data in the lab is fictional, but this type of bias occurs in real AI systems.
